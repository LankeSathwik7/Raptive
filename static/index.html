The big question: Is there a connection between how long someone stays on a page and the revenue that visit produces? Let's find out.

First Look
Let's Plot Every Visit

We'll start simple. We'll plot every visit on a chart with time on the X axis and revenue on the Y axis, then see if there's a pattern. Each dot is one page visit. The dashed red line is the overall trend.

What it is

A scatter plot puts a dot for each data point. The position tells us two things at once: how long they stayed (horizontal) and how much revenue (vertical).

Why we chose it

When we want to see if two numbers are related, scatter plots are the go-to. We can instantly spot patterns. Are the dots going up? Down? Scattered randomly?

Why not a bar chart?

Bar charts are for comparing categories (like Chrome vs Safari). We have 4,000 continuous data points, so bars would be meaningless here.

Why not a line chart?

Line charts connect points in order, implying a sequence (like stock prices over time). Our data isn't sequential. Each dot is an independent visit.

The red dashed line

That's the "best fit line," also known as a regression line. If we had to draw ONE line through all the dots, this is where it goes. Notice: it's sloping down.

Huh. The line goes down. That seems to say more time on page = less money. The correlation is -0.56, which is moderately strong and statistically significant (the p-value is less than 0.001). Case closed? Not so fast…
The Twist
Wait… Something Smells Fishy

Here's a famous brain teaser: Ice cream sales and drowning deaths go up at the same time. Does ice cream cause drowning?

Of course not. It's hot weather. People eat more ice cream AND swim more when it's hot. That "hidden factor" is called a confounding variable. And our data has them too.

Here's what's happening in our data: Mobile users spend MORE time on pages… but generate LESS revenue. Safari users? Same story. So when we mash everyone together, it looks like time hurts revenue. But really, it's the device and browser doing the heavy lifting.

See the paradox? Mobile users stay 2.4x longer but earn 34% less revenue. When we mix all the groups together, this imbalance makes it look like time hurts revenue. But it's actually the type of user that matters.

Breaking It Apart
What Happens When We Separate the Groups?

What if we look at each group separately? Chrome desktop users only. Safari mobile users only. Does time still hurt revenue?

What it is

Same scatter plot idea, but split into 4 mini-charts, one for each browser+platform combo. This technique is called "faceting" or "small multiples."

Why we chose it

When we suspect a hidden variable is messing with our results, the fix is to separate the groups and look at each one individually. If the pattern changes, that's our answer. We found the culprit.

Why not one chart with colors?

We show that in Chart 1 (with the toggleable legend). But small multiples make it impossible to miss because each group gets its own clean space.

The red lines

Each mini-chart has its own trend line. Notice something? They all slope up or stay nearly flat. Within each group, time on page doesn't hurt revenue!

Look at that. When we stop mixing apples and oranges, the story flips. Within each group, more time either helps or is neutral. The overall "negative" trend was an illusion created by mixing fundamentally different user segments.
The Big Reveal
Putting a Number on the Flip

Our statistical model calculated the exact "effect" of time on revenue: once without controlling for anything, and once controlling for browser and platform. Look at what happens.

What it is

A bar chart comparing two coefficients. It looks simple, but the story it tells is anything but.

Why we chose it

Sometimes the most powerful chart is the simplest one. Two bars. One red (negative), one green (positive). The visual contrast makes Simpson's Paradox impossible to miss.

Why not a scatter here?

We already showed the scatter. This is about ONE number, the coefficient, and how it flips sign. A bar chart makes that comparison crisp.

The zero line

That dashed line at zero is the boundary. Below it = time hurts revenue. Above it = time helps revenue. We went from below to above just by accounting for confounders.

This is Simpson's Paradox, a real statistical phenomenon where a trend reverses when we split the data into meaningful groups. It happens more often than we'd expect, and it's exactly why jumping to conclusions from surface-level data is dangerous.

The unadjusted coefficient was -0.000195 (negative, meaning more time = less revenue). After controlling for browser and platform, it flipped to +0.000097 (positive, meaning more time = slightly more revenue), and this result is statistically significant. The model with controls has an R² of 85.1%, meaning it explains the vast majority of revenue variation. We can be confident in this finding because the confidence interval for the adjusted coefficient does not cross zero.

Understanding the Shape
How Is Our Data Distributed?

Before we wrap up, let's understand the shape of our data. How is revenue distributed? Where do most people fall? How different are the segments really?

Histograms (top row)

These show how many data points fall into each range. Think of it like sorting people by height into buckets. Most people end up in the middle, with fewer at the extremes. Our revenue histogram shows a shape centered around ~$0.011.

Why histograms?

When we want to see the "shape" of our data (is it normal? skewed? does it have multiple peaks?), a histogram is the answer.

Box plots (bottom row)

Box plots pack a lot of info into a small space. Here's how to read them, piece by piece:

  • The line in the middle of the box is the median (the value right in the center if we sorted all data points from smallest to largest).
  • The bottom edge of the box is Q1 (the 25th percentile). 25% of values fall below this line.
  • The top edge of the box is Q3 (the 75th percentile). 75% of values fall below this line.
  • The box itself (Q1 to Q3) contains the middle 50% of all data points. This range is called the Interquartile Range (IQR).
  • The lines extending above and below (called "whiskers") show the rest of the spread, reaching out to the most extreme values that aren't considered outliers.
  • Any individual dots beyond the whiskers are outliers, values that are unusually far from the rest.

So at a glance, we can compare the entire distribution of two groups. In our charts, we can immediately see the revenue gap between Chrome and Safari, or Desktop and Mobile.

Why box plots instead of more histograms?

We're comparing groups (Chrome vs Safari, Desktop vs Mobile). Box plots let us see two distributions side-by-side cleanly. The differences jump right out.

So What?
What Should We Do About This?

Great analysis is useless without action. Here are the key takeaways and what they mean in practice.

High Priority
Segment-Aware Optimization
Don't chase engagement time blindly. Design separate strategies for mobile vs desktop users. What works for Chrome desktop visitors may backfire on Safari mobile.
High Priority
Mobile Monetization Deep Dive
Mobile generates ~34% less revenue per visit. Investigate root causes: ad format issues, load times, or traffic quality differences.
Medium Priority
Safari User Analysis
Safari users generate ~22% less revenue. Check for Intelligent Tracking Prevention impact, ad blocking rates, and demographic differences.
High Priority
A/B Testing Framework
Test engagement features by segment. Use stratified sampling to ensure fair comparisons. Measure if increased time lifts revenue within each group.
Medium Priority
Predictive Segmentation Model
Build real-time user value prediction at session start. Personalize content and ad strategies based on predicted value. Serve premium experiences to high-value segments and adjust ad density dynamically.
The bottom line: Revenue optimization must be segment-specific. Platform and browser type matter far more than raw engagement time. Always control for confounders before drawing conclusions.
